Theorems and Skills for Differential Equations

Separation of Variables

If possible re-write $\frac{dx}{dt}= f(x,t)$ as $u(x) dx = v(t) dt$ Given a differential equation, posit a solution of the form $\phi(t)\psi(x)$, plug in and use properties of the equation to determine $\phi, \psi$

Picard's Theorem

If $f:\mathbb{R}^{n} \times \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ is Lipschitz on a neighborhood $U = \{(x,t) : |x-x_{0}|< b, |t-t_{0}| < a\}$ then $$\frac{dx}{dt} = f(x,t),\ f(t_{0}) = x_{0}$$ has a unique solution on the inteveral $\displaystyle |t-t_{0}| < \min(a, {b \over \sup_{U} |f|})$.

Integrating Factor

To solve $y' + P(x)y = Q(x)$ multiply by $\displaystyle e^{\int P(x) dx}$ and integrate

Implicit Function Theorem

Suppose $U \subset \mathbb{R}^{n} \times \mathbb{R}^{m}$ and $f: U \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{m}$ is $C^{r}$. Then for any $(x_{0},y_{0}) \in U$ there is a neighborhood, $V$ of $z_{0} = f((x_{0},y_{0}))$ such that $f$ is the graph of a unique $C^{r}$ function $y = g(x)$ on $V$. Moreover, the partial derivatives of $y$ are given by $${dy \over dx_{i}} = {{\partial f \over \partial x_{i} }\over {\partial f \over \partial y}}$$

Solution of Differential Equations with Characteristic Equation

If $r$ is a solution to the characteristic equation of a linear homogenous differential equation then $Ae^{rt}$ is a solution. If $r$ is a repeated root then the solution must be multiplied by appropriate powers of $x$. If $r = a+ib$ is complex then the solution is $e^{at}(A \cos t + B \sin t)$.

Method of Undetermined Coefficients

To solve $ay'' + by' + cy = G(x)$ set $G(x) =0$ to find complementary solution then use the form of $G(x)$ to guess a particular solution of the same form. If you get a particular solution equal a complmentary solution, multiply the particular solution by an appropriate power of $x$.

Rayleigh's (Plancharel's) Theorem

If $\tilde{f}$ is the fourier transform of $f: \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, $f$ is sufficiently smooth and decays sufficiently quickly then $$\int_{\mathbb{R}} |f(x)|^2dx = \int_{\mathbb{R}} |\tilde{f}(t)|^2 dt$$
